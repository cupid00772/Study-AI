## 七. Reading Comprehension (Questions 21-25)
Instructions: Read the following article and answer the questions that follow.

**The Hidden Costs of Fast Fashion**
In the last two decades, the fashion industry has undergone a radical transformation. The rise of "fast fashion"—a business model built on rapidly producing high volumes of clothing to replicate current trends at low prices—has democratized style. Consumers can now purchase trendy outfits for a fraction of what they would have cost in the past. Brands like Zara, H&M, and Shein release thousands of new styles weekly, encouraging a culture of constant consumption. However, this seemingly beneficial accessibility masks a multitude of hidden costs, primarily environmental and ethical.

Environmentally, fast fashion is devastating. The fashion industry is responsible for approximately 10% of global carbon emissions, more than all international flights and maritime shipping combined. The production of cheap garments relies heavily on synthetic fibers like polyester, which are derived from fossil fuels and take hundreds of years to decompose. Furthermore, textile dyeing is the second largest polluter of water globally. When clothes are washed, they shed microplastics into the ocean, infiltrating marine ecosystems and, ultimately, the human food chain. 

On the ethical front, the drive to keep retail prices rock-bottom places immense pressure on the supply chain. To maintain profit margins, brands outsource manufacturing to developing countries with lax labor laws. Garment workers, predominantly women, often endure grueling hours in unsafe conditions for wages that fall well below a living standard. The tragic collapse of the Rana Plaza factory in Bangladesh in 2013, which killed over 1,100 workers, temporarily brought these issues to light, yet systemic exploitation remains widespread.

Despite growing awareness, consumer behavior is slow to change. The psychological thrill of buying something new and affordable often overrides ethical considerations. While "sustainable fashion" movements have gained traction, advocating for buying less, choosing quality over quantity, and supporting ethical brands, these alternatives are frequently criticized for being elitist due to their higher price points. 

True reform in the fashion industry will require a multi-faceted approach. Governments must enforce stricter environmental and labor regulations, holding multinational corporations accountable for their supply chains. Simultaneously, consumers need to shift their mindset from viewing clothing as disposable to treating it as a long-term investment. Until this paradigm shift occurs, the true price of that cheap t-shirt will continue to be paid by the planet and its most vulnerable populations.
